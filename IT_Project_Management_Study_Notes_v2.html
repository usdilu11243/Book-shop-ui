Week 1
Introduction to IT Project Management
What projects are, key attributes, constraints, and process groups
Summary
A project is a temporary endeavor to create a unique product, service, or result. IT projects span from small hardware installs to large multi-year software systems. Project management applies knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to meet project requirements. The five PMBOK process groups are Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring & Controlling, and Closing.
Key Points
  • A project has a unique purpose, is temporary, drives change, and involves uncertainty
  • The triple constraint: Scope, Time, and Cost — all must be balanced
  • A program = related projects managed together for benefits not available individually
  • A portfolio = projects/programs managed as a group to achieve strategic objectives
  • IT projects need strong governance to avoid cost/schedule overruns
  • IT teams must recognize and stop failing projects early
  • Project challenges: Issues, Risks, Assumptions, Constraints
Exam Q&A
What is a project? ▼
A temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result. It has a definite beginning and end, delivers value, and involves uncertainty.
What are the three elements of the triple constraint? ▼
Scope, Time, and Cost. Every project is constrained by these three, and balancing them is a core project management responsibility.
List the five PMBOK process groups in order. ▼
1. Initiating, 2. Planning, 3. Executing, 4. Monitoring & Controlling, 5. Closing.
What is the difference between a program and a portfolio? ▼
A program is a group of related projects managed together to gain benefits not available individually. A portfolio is a collection of projects, programs, and operations managed as a group to achieve strategic organizational objectives.
Name four key attributes of a project. ▼
A project has a unique purpose, is temporary, drives change, requires resources from various areas, involves uncertainty, has a primary customer or sponsor, and is developed using progressive elaboration.
Week 2
Project Management Methodologies
Waterfall, Agile, Scrum, Kanban, Lean, CPM, CCM, PRINCE2
Summary
A project management methodology is a structured framework guiding how a project is planned, executed, monitored, and completed. Traditional (Waterfall) uses upfront planning and one final deliverable. Agile is iterative with continuous delivery. Various methodologies suit different project types: Scrum (software), Kanban (continuous flow), Lean (waste reduction), CPM (scheduling), CCM (resource constraints), PRINCE2 (government/enterprise).
Key Points
  • Waterfall phases: Requirements → Design → Implementation → Verification → Maintenance
  • Waterfall suits projects with fixed, stable, well-defined requirements
  • Agile values: Individuals & interactions, Working solutions, Customer collaboration, Responding to change
  • Scrum uses sprints (1–4 weeks), daily stand-ups, product backlog, sprint reviews
  • Kanban uses a board with To Do / In Progress / Done columns; limits WIP
  • Lean originated at Toyota; focuses on eliminating waste and maximizing value
  • CPM identifies the critical path — the longest sequence of dependent tasks
  • CCM extends CPM by considering resource availability and adding buffers
  • PRINCE2 = Projects IN Controlled Environments; widely used outside the US
Exam Q&A
What are the five phases of the Waterfall methodology? ▼
Requirements, Design, Implementation, Verification, and Maintenance.
What are the four core values of the Agile Manifesto? ▼
1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools. 2. Working software over comprehensive documentation. 3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation. 4. Responding to change over following a fixed plan.
What is the Critical Path Method (CPM)? ▼
A scheduling technique that identifies the longest sequence of dependent tasks that must be completed to finish a project on time. Any delay in critical path tasks directly delays the project.
How does Critical Chain Management (CCM) differ from CPM? ▼
CCM focuses on resource availability (not just task duration) and adds strategic buffers at key points in the schedule to protect against delays from resource shortages or uncertainty.
What does PRINCE2 stand for and when is it used? ▼
Projects IN Controlled Environments. It is a process-based methodology used in 150+ countries, suitable for complex but predictable projects requiring clear roles and formal governance — especially government and large-scale IT projects.
What are the key Kanban practices? ▼
Visualize the workflow, limit work in progress (WIP), manage flow, make policies explicit, use feedback loops, and improve collaboratively.
Week 3
Foundations of Agile Project Management
Agile manifesto, Scrum roles, Kanban board, other Agile frameworks
Summary
Agile was introduced in 2001 by 17 software developers. It is a mindset guided by the Agile Manifesto, not a single methodology. Agile delivers value early through iterative cycles. Scrum is the most popular Agile framework, using sprints and defined roles. Kanban visualizes work and limits WIP for continuous delivery. Other frameworks include XP, FDD, Lean, and Crystal.
Key Points
  • Agile development cycle: Requirements → Plan → Design → Develop → Test → Deploy → Monitor (iterative)
  • Scrum roles: Product Owner (backlog/vision), Scrum Master (facilitates/removes blockers), Scrum Team (self-organizing, cross-functional)
  • Scrum events: Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum (15 min), Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective
  • Scrum artifacts: Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Burndown Chart
  • Kanban originated from Toyota Production System; focuses on continuous delivery
  • Agile advantages: embraces change, faster delivery, customer involvement, continuous feedback
  • Agile disadvantages: less predictable planning, requires skilled team, limited documentation risk
Exam Q&A
What are the three Scrum roles? ▼
Product Owner (defines vision, prioritizes backlog), Scrum Master (facilitates process, removes obstacles), and the Scrum Team (cross-functional, self-organizing, responsible for delivery).
What are the four Scrum events? ▼
Sprint Planning (select work), Daily Scrum (15-minute stand-up), Sprint Review (demonstrate completed work to stakeholders), Sprint Retrospective (team reflects and improves).
What is a Burndown Chart? ▼
A Scrum artifact that visualizes the amount of remaining work over time within a sprint, helping teams track progress toward their sprint goal.
Name three advantages and two disadvantages of Agile. ▼
Advantages: embraces change, faster delivery through short iterations, strong customer involvement. Disadvantages: less predictable planning/delivery dates, requires highly skilled and experienced team members.
How does Kanban differ from Scrum? ▼
Scrum uses fixed-length sprints with defined roles and ceremonies. Kanban is a continuous flow system with no fixed iterations — it focuses on visualizing work, limiting WIP, and minimizing cycle time without fixed-length development cycles.
Week 4
Project Initiation
Initiating Process Group, Project Charter, Stakeholder Identification
Summary
Project initiation formally starts a project and answers why it exists, who is involved, and who has authority. The Initiating Process Group contains two key processes: Develop Project Charter and Identify Stakeholders. The Project Charter authorizes the project, grants PM authority, and links the project to organizational strategy. The Stakeholder Register documents all stakeholders and their interests, power, and influence.
Key Points
  • Project Charter inputs: Business case, Benefits management plan, Agreements (contracts/MOUs), EEFs, OPAs
  • Project Charter contents: Purpose, objectives, high-level scope, milestone schedule, budget, risks, stakeholder list, PM authority
  • Tools for Develop Project Charter: Expert judgment, Data gathering (brainstorming, interviews), Facilitation
  • Business case explains reason, expected value, cost-benefit justification, and strategic alignment
  • Project Sponsor: approves charter, provides financial/org support, resolves escalations
  • Stakeholder analysis models: Power–Interest Grid, Power–Influence Grid, Influence–Impact Matrix
  • Stakeholder Register contains: name/role, contact info, influence/interest level, expectations, classification
  • EEFs = Enterprise Environmental Factors (external); OPAs = Organizational Process Assets (internal)
Exam Q&A
What are the two processes in the Initiating Process Group? ▼
1. Develop Project Charter, and 2. Identify Stakeholders.
What is the purpose of the Project Charter? ▼
It formally authorizes the project, grants authority to the project manager, defines high-level scope and objectives, links the project to organizational strategy, and serves as a foundation for planning.
What are EEFs and OPAs? Give examples. ▼
EEFs (Enterprise Environmental Factors) are external conditions influencing the project — e.g., market conditions, government regulations. OPAs (Organizational Process Assets) are internal resources — e.g., templates, historical data, lessons learned.
What does the Stakeholder Register contain? ▼
Stakeholder name and role, contact information, influence and interest level, expectations and requirements, and classification/priority. It is continuously updated throughout the project.
Why is early stakeholder identification important? ▼
Failure to identify stakeholders early can cause resistance to change, missed requirements, delays, rework, and project failure. Early identification improves communication, decision-making, and stakeholder buy-in.
What is the role of the Project Sponsor? ▼
The sponsor provides financial and organizational support, approves the project charter, resolves escalated issues, and champions the project at senior management levels. Strong sponsorship is a critical success factor.
Week 5
Project Schedule Management & CPM
Scheduling processes, network diagrams, critical path, float calculations
Summary
Project schedule management ensures timely completion through six processes: Plan Schedule Management, Define Activities, Sequence Activities, Estimate Activity Durations, Develop Schedule, and Control Schedule. The Critical Path Method (CPM) identifies the longest path in a network and calculates ES, EF, LS, LF, Total Float, and Free Float. AOA and AON are the two network diagram types.
Key Points
  • Six Schedule Management processes: Plan → Define → Sequence → Estimate → Develop → Control
  • Activity relationships: Finish-to-Start (FS), Finish-to-Finish (FF), Start-to-Start (SS), Start-to-Finish (SF)
  • Lead = time a successor activity can start early; Lag = delay added between activities
  • Critical Path = longest sequence of activities; determines minimum project duration; total float = 0
  • Forward pass calculates ES and EF; Backward pass calculates LS and LF
  • Total Float = LF − EF or LS − ES (can delay activity without delaying project)
  • Free Float = ES of successor − EF of activity (can delay without affecting successor)
  • Dummy activity in AOA: zero duration, used only to show dependencies
  • Fulkerson's Rule: number nodes by removing arrows iteratively from initial events
Exam Q&A
What is the Critical Path and why does it matter? ▼
The critical path is the longest sequence of dependent activities in a project network. It determines the shortest possible project duration. Any delay on the critical path directly delays the project finish date.
Define Total Float and Free Float. Give their formulas. ▼
Total Float = the amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the project. Formula: TF = LF − EF (or LS − ES). Free Float = the time an activity can be delayed without affecting the early start of its successor. Formula: FF = ES(successor) − EF(activity).
What are the four types of activity relationships in CPM? ▼
Finish-to-Start, Finish-to-Finish, Start-to-Start, and Start-to-Finish. FS is most common — successor cannot start until predecessor finishes.
What is the difference between Lead and Lag? ▼
Lead is the amount of time a successor activity can be advanced (started early) relative to its predecessor. Lag is the amount of required delay before the successor can start after the predecessor finishes.
What is a dummy activity in an AOA network? ▼
A dummy activity requires no time or resources (duration = 0). It is used in AOA networks only to correctly represent dependencies between activities and to ensure each activity has a unique identity.
What does total float = 0 signify? ▼
Activities with total float = 0 are on the critical path. They have no scheduling flexibility — any delay will directly delay the project completion date.
Week 6
Project Resource Management
Planning, allocating, and controlling human, financial, and physical resources
Summary
Project resource management focuses on ensuring the right resources are available at the right time. It covers human, financial, physical resources, and tools. Resource capacity planning prevents overcommitment. Key techniques include resource leveling (adjust schedules) and resource smoothing (redistribute within deadlines). Effective resource management prevents delays, controls costs, and improves productivity.
Key Points
  • Types of resources: Human, Financial, Physical, Time/Capacity, Tools & Technology
  • Main components: Planning → Acquisition → Allocation → Scheduling → Monitoring → Optimization → Conflict Resolution → Performance Evaluation → Release
  • Resource Leveling: adjust schedules to reduce overallocation conflicts
  • Resource Smoothing: redistribute work within fixed deadlines
  • Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS) used in planning
  • Key tools: Workload/Capacity views, Resource Calendars, Time Tracking, Forecasting Dashboards
  • Common challenges: shortages, overallocation, unclear roles, poor communication, scope changes
Exam Q&A
What is project resource management? ▼
The process of planning, allocating, and controlling resources (human, financial, physical) to meet project objectives efficiently. It ensures the right resources are available at the right time and used optimally.
What is the difference between resource leveling and resource smoothing? ▼
Resource leveling adjusts the project schedule to resolve resource conflicts and overallocation — it may extend the project duration. Resource smoothing redistributes work within the existing schedule constraints without changing the project end date.
Name five types of project resources. ▼
Human Resources (team members/experts), Financial Resources (budget/funding), Physical Resources (equipment/materials), Time and Capacity (available working time), and Tools and Technology (software/systems).
What is resource capacity in project management? ▼
Resource capacity is the amount of work that people, tools, and equipment can realistically handle within a set period. Managing capacity prevents overcommitment and burnout, and ensures tasks are completed efficiently.
Week 7
Project Cost Management
Estimation, budgeting, cost control, and Earned Value Management (EVM)
Summary
Project Cost Management ensures costs stay within budget through four steps: Resource Planning, Cost Estimation, Cost Budgeting, and Cost Control. Estimation techniques include analogous, parametric, PERT, top-down, and bottom-up methods. The cost baseline enables performance measurement. Earned Value Management (EVM) uses PV, EV, and AC to measure schedule and cost performance.
Key Points
  • Four steps: Resource Planning → Cost Estimation → Cost Budgeting → Cost Control
  • Estimation techniques: Analogous, Parametric, PERT, Top-down, Bottom-up
  • Costing methods: Activity-Based Costing, Job Costing, Lifecycle Costing
  • Lifecycle Costing considers development + operation + maintenance + replacement costs
  • Value Engineering: optimize function vs cost, improve value without reducing quality
  • Cost Baseline = allocated budget across tasks and time; used for variance tracking
  • EVM: PV = Planned Value, EV = Earned Value, AC = Actual Cost
  • Schedule Variance (SV) = EV − PV; Cost Variance (CV) = EV − AC
  • SV negative = behind schedule; CV positive = under budget
Exam Q&A
What are the four steps of project cost management? ▼
1. Resource Planning (identify required resources), 2. Cost Estimation (quantify costs), 3. Cost Budgeting (allocate costs across tasks/time), 4. Cost Control (monitor and take corrective action).
Explain EVM and its three key metrics. ▼
Earned Value Management (EVM) measures project performance. PV (Planned Value) = budgeted cost for planned work; EV (Earned Value) = budgeted cost for work actually performed; AC (Actual Cost) = actual cost incurred for work performed.
How do you calculate Schedule Variance and Cost Variance? ▼
Schedule Variance (SV) = EV − PV. If negative, the project is behind schedule. Cost Variance (CV) = EV − AC. If positive, the project is under budget; if negative, it is over budget.
EVM Example: Task budget $4,000, planned 50% done, actually 25% done, AC = $800. Calculate PV, EV, SV, CV. ▼
PV = 50% × $4,000 = $2,000. EV = 25% × $4,000 = $1,000. SV = EV − PV = $1,000 − $2,000 = −$1,000 (behind schedule). CV = EV − AC = $1,000 − $800 = +$200 (under budget).
What is lifecycle costing? ▼
A costing methodology that considers the full lifecycle of a product or system including development, operation, maintenance, and replacement costs — not just initial project costs. It encourages long-term financial thinking.
What is the role of the cost baseline? ▼
The cost baseline is the approved budget distributed across tasks and time. It enables performance measurement, allows variance tracking, and supports phased funding decisions. Without a baseline, there is no control benchmark.
Week 8
Quality Assurance in IT Projects
QA vs QC, audits, tools, software QA, and the future of QA
Summary
Quality Assurance (QA) audits quality requirements and control measurements to ensure appropriate standards are used. QA creates systems to measure and control quality (proactive); Quality Control measures individual deliverables (reactive). QA audits involve root cause analysis, preventative actions, and process improvements. AI/ML are increasingly used in QA for predictive maintenance and anomaly detection.
Key Points
  • QA = proactive system to ensure quality processes; QC = reactive measurement of individual deliverables
  • QA audit activities: analyze QC data, identify improvements, root cause analysis, determine preventative actions
  • QA tools: Affinity diagrams, Process decision charts, Interrelationship diagrams, Tree diagrams, Prioritization matrices, Network diagrams, Matrix diagrams
  • Importance of QA: Customer satisfaction, Public trust, Product quality (prevents defects proactively)
  • Software QA (SQA): systematic approach to identify patterns and improve development processes
  • SQA career roles: SQA Engineer, Analyst, Manager, Test Automation Specialist
  • Future of QA: AI/ML for predictive maintenance, real-time anomaly detection, low-code/no-code tools
Exam Q&A
What is the difference between QA and QC? ▼
QA (Quality Assurance) creates systems and processes to measure and control quality — it is proactive and aims to prevent defects. QC (Quality Control) measures the quality of individual products or deliverables and accepts or rejects them — it is reactive.
What activities are included in a QA audit? ▼
Analyzing quality control data to identify problems, identifying process improvements to increase quality, performing root cause analysis to determine necessary improvements, and determining preventative actions to deter future quality issues.
Why is QA important for an organization? ▼
QA builds customer satisfaction by meeting needs and expectations, builds public trust and loyalty, and prevents product defects through proactive standards and procedures. It also facilitates improvement in quality processes overall.
How are AI and ML being used in QA? ▼
AI and ML are used to inspect products and monitor machinery performance, support predictive maintenance by identifying potential problems before failures occur, analyze real-time data to detect patterns and anomalies, and help identify quality issues early before they become major defects.
Week 9
Project Risk Management
Risk types, attitudes, appetite, probability/impact, and risk lifecycle
Summary
Project risk is an uncertain event that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives. Positive risks are opportunities; negative risks are threats. Risk management follows a lifecycle: Plan → Identify → Qualitative Analysis → Quantitative Analysis → Plan Responses → Implement Responses → Monitor. Seven PMI principles guide risk management practice.
Key Points
  • Risk value = Probability × Impact
  • Risk Attitude: ranges from risk averse to risk seeking; varies by situation
  • Risk Appetite: degree of uncertainty an organization is willing to accept for a reward (PMI)
  • Risk Threshold: measure of acceptable variation before escalation is triggered
  • Four risk categories: Known-Known (facts), Known-Unknown (classic risk), Unknown-Known (hidden fact), Unknown-Unknown (emergent)
  • 7 PMI Risk Principles: Excellence, Organizational alignment, Focus on impactful risks, Balance value vs risk, Foster risk culture, Navigate complexity, Continuous improvement
  • Risk Lifecycle: Plan → Identify → Qualitative Analysis → Quantitative Analysis → Plan Responses → Implement → Monitor
Exam Q&A
What is a project risk? How do positive and negative risks differ? ▼
A project risk is an uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives. Positive risks are called opportunities (beneficial outcomes). Negative risks are called threats (harmful outcomes).
What are the four categories of risk? ▼
Known-Known (facts and requirements you are aware of), Known-Unknown (classic risk — you know it might happen but not whether it will), Unknown-Known (hidden fact — something that is true but unrecognized), Unknown-Unknown (emergent — completely unforeseen).
What is the difference between risk appetite and risk threshold? ▼
Risk appetite is the degree of uncertainty an organization is willing to accept in anticipation of a reward — it reflects strategic willingness to take risk. Risk threshold is the specific measure of acceptable variation around an objective before an escalation is triggered — it is the operational boundary.
List the seven steps in the Risk Management Lifecycle. ▼
1. Plan Risk Management, 2. Identify Risks, 3. Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis, 4. Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis, 5. Plan Risk Responses, 6. Implement Risk Responses, 7. Monitor Risks.
What are the seven PMI Risk Management Principles? ▼
1. Strive for excellence in risk management practice. 2. Align with organizational strategy. 3. Focus on the most impactful risks. 4. Balance value realization against overall risk. 5. Foster a culture that embraces risk management. 6. Navigate complexity using risk management. 7. Continuously improve risk management competencies.
How is risk value calculated? ▼
Risk Value = Probability × Impact. Probability is the likelihood of the risk occurring (0%–100%). Impact is the positive or negative consequence if the risk occurs. Multiplying them gives an overall risk measure for prioritization.
Week 10
Effective Communication in Projects
Communication process, types, management plan, barriers, and best practices
Summary
Communication is described as the "lifeblood" of project management. Project managers spend ~90% of their time communicating. The communication process includes Sender, Message, Medium, Receiver, Feedback, and Noise. Only 7% of communication is verbal; 55% is non-verbal. A Communication Management Plan defines what, who, when, how, and escalation procedures.
Key Points
  • Lasswell's model: Who says what, To whom, Through which channel, With what effect
  • Communication breakdown: 7% verbal, 38% tone/voice, 55% non-verbal
  • Types: Verbal (meetings), Written (emails/reports), Non-verbal (body language)
  • Communication Management Plan defines: what info, who needs it, when/how, methods, escalation
  • Steps for effective communication: identify requirements, define 5Ws+1H, consider environmental factors, establish feedback mechanisms
  • Common barriers: information overload, cultural differences, language issues, lack of clarity, remote work challenges
  • Overcoming barriers: simple language, active listening, open culture, adapt to audience, right tools
  • Face-to-face is best for complex/sensitive issues; email for routine updates
Exam Q&A
What percentage of communication is verbal vs non-verbal? ▼
Only 7% of communication is verbal. 38% depends on tone and voice. 55% is non-verbal (body language, gestures). This highlights the importance of face-to-face communication for complex or sensitive discussions.
What are the six elements of the communication process? ▼
Sender (initiates message), Message (information being communicated), Medium (channel used), Receiver (interprets the message), Feedback (confirms understanding), Noise (any distortion affecting the message).
What does a Communication Management Plan define? ▼
It defines what information should be communicated, who needs the information, when and how communication occurs, communication methods (emails, meetings, reports), and escalation procedures.
What are the main barriers to effective communication in projects? ▼
Information overload, cultural differences, language and terminology issues, lack of clarity in messages, and remote work challenges. These can lead to misunderstandings, delays, and conflicts if not managed.
What is Lasswell's communication model? ▼
Lasswell's model describes communication as: Who says What, To Whom, Through Which Channel, With What Effect. In project management this means communication must be purposeful, structured, and outcome-oriented — the right information to the right person at the right time.
Week 11
Leadership in Project Management
Leadership styles, skills, theories, and the leader vs manager distinction
Summary
Leadership in project management is a soft skill combining art and science — it focuses on achieving results through people. A project leader differs from a manager: leadership inspires and focuses on vision/people, while management plans, organizes, and controls tasks/processes. Leadership styles include Transformational, Transactional, Servant, and Adaptive. Effective leadership improves team motivation, decision-making, and project success.
Key Points
  • Leadership roles: define vision, inspire/motivate, facilitate decisions, manage risks, ensure communication
  • Leader vs Manager: Leadership = inspires, people/vision focused; Management = plans/controls, task/process focused
  • Key leadership skills: Communication, Adaptability, Delegation, Decision-making, Problem-solving, Time management
  • Additional competencies: Risk evaluation, Analytical thinking, Building cohesion, Motivating, Creating urgency, Delivering results
  • Transformational: inspires vision, innovation, team growth, strong commitment
  • Transactional: rewards/penalties, structure and control, ensures task completion
  • Servant: prioritizes team needs, builds trust and collaboration, enhances morale
  • Leadership matrix quadrants: Reactive People, Reactive Task, Proactive People, Proactive Task
  • Leadership theories: Great Man, Transformational, Adaptive, Strengths-Based, Leader-Member Exchange
Exam Q&A
How does a project leader differ from a project manager? ▼
A project leader inspires and motivates — focused on people and vision. A project manager plans, organizes, and controls — focused on tasks and processes. Effective project leaders combine both, but leadership is about achieving results through others.
Describe the three main leadership styles covered in this module. ▼
Transformational: inspires vision and innovation, focuses on team growth and commitment. Transactional: based on rewards and penalties, focuses on structure, control, and task completion. Servant: prioritizes team needs over the leader's own, builds trust, collaboration, and morale.
What are six key skills of a project leader? ▼
Communication, Adaptability, Delegation, Decision-making, Problem-solving, and Time management. Additional competencies include risk evaluation, analytical thinking, building team cohesion, motivating people, creating urgency, and delivering results.
Why is leadership important in project management? ▼
Leadership motivates team performance, builds trust and collaboration, ensures clear communication, helps manage uncertainty and change, and improves stakeholder satisfaction. Strong leadership directly contributes to achieving project goals.
What are some "do's and don'ts" of leading by example? ▼
Do: support the team and vision, follow through on commitments, be punctual and responsible. Don't: take all credit for team success, blame others for failures, or ignore team contributions.
What is the Leadership Matrix? ▼
A framework with two dimensions — Reactive vs Proactive, and People-oriented vs Task-oriented — forming four quadrants: Reactive-People, Reactive-Task, Proactive-People, and Proactive-Task. Proactive-People leadership is generally considered the most effective style in project environments.
Week 12
Monitoring and Controlling Projects
Tracking performance, baselines, change management, risk control, and quality assurance
Summary
Monitoring and controlling is a continuous process throughout project execution that involves tracking performance, comparing with plans, and taking corrective actions to ensure project objectives are achieved. Monitoring means observing and collecting data (Check), while controlling means making decisions and fixing deviations (Act). Together they form a feedback loop: Measure → Compare → Identify Deviations → Correct. Key areas include scope, schedule, and cost baselines; change management; risk monitoring; quality control; and performance measurement using KPIs.
Key Points
  • Monitoring = Check (observe, collect data, identify deviations); Controlling = Act (analyze, decide, fix)
  • The feedback loop: Measure performance → Compare with plan → Identify deviations → Take corrective action
  • Three baselines used for comparison: Scope baseline, Schedule baseline, Cost baseline
  • Key monitoring activities: performance measurement, milestone tracking, risk monitoring, resource tracking, quality checking, documentation
  • Key control activities: issue resolution, risk mitigation, change management, resource reallocation, cost & schedule adjustments
  • Change management: all changes must be documented, evaluated, and approved to prevent scope creep
  • Risk monitoring: identify risks early, maintain a risk log, apply mitigation strategies
  • Performance measured using KPIs: Time, Cost, Quality — planned vs actual comparison
  • Technology role: dashboards, Gantt charts, real-time data tracking, automated reporting, custom PM tools
  • Real-world analogy: "Driving a vehicle" — monitoring is checking speed/fuel/direction; controlling is steering/braking/accelerating
  • Challenges: poor data accuracy, communication gaps, scope creep, resistance to change, resource limitations
  • Solutions: reliable tools, better communication, change control systems, team training
Exam Q&A
What is the difference between monitoring and controlling? ▼
Monitoring is the "Check" phase — it involves observing progress, collecting data, and identifying deviations from the plan. Controlling is the "Act" phase — it involves making decisions, taking corrective actions, and fixing deviations. Both work together in a continuous feedback loop to keep the project on track.
What are the three project baselines used in monitoring and control? ▼
1. Scope baseline — defines what is and is not included in the project. 2. Schedule baseline — the approved timeline against which actual progress is measured. 3. Cost baseline — the approved budget against which actual expenditure is compared. All three are used to evaluate planned vs actual performance.
Describe the monitoring and control feedback loop. ▼
The feedback loop has four steps: (1) Measure performance — collect data on project progress. (2) Compare with plan — assess actual against the baseline. (3) Identify deviations — determine where the project is off track. (4) Take corrective action — implement changes to realign with the plan. The loop repeats continuously throughout project execution.
Why is change management important in monitoring and control? ▼
Changes are inevitable in projects, but uncontrolled changes lead to scope creep — where the project grows beyond its original boundaries, consuming extra time and cost. A formal change management process ensures every change is documented, evaluated for impact, and formally approved before implementation, keeping the project within agreed constraints.
What are the key objectives of monitoring and controlling? ▼
Ensure the project stays on schedule, control costs, maintain quality standards, manage and mitigate risks, and improve decision-making. Collectively these objectives help ensure that project goals are achieved within the approved scope, time, and budget.
List five common challenges in monitoring and controlling and their solutions. ▼
Challenges: (1) Poor data accuracy, (2) Communication gaps, (3) Scope creep, (4) Resistance to change, (5) Resource limitations. Solutions: (1) Use reliable and automated tools, (2) Improve stakeholder communication, (3) Implement a formal change control system, (4) Train project teams on processes and change culture, (5) Proactively plan and reallocate resources as needed.
How does technology support monitoring and controlling? ▼
Technology enables real-time data tracking through dashboards and project management software, automates status reporting, visualizes schedules via Gantt charts, and integrates with databases for up-to-date information. Organizations can also build custom PM tools using low-code/no-code platforms, offering flexibility and cost efficiency tailored to their needs.
